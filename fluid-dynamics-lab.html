Fluids Lab: Observing the Effect of Artery Blockage Through Flow Visualization

Overview

This project aimed to simulate and analyze the effects of artery blockages on blood flow using a soap film experiment. By visualizing flow patterns and calculating pressure differences, we sought to provide insights into the risks associated with arterial blockages, such as strokes and heart attacks. The experiment utilized 3D-printed models to represent healthy and unhealthy artery conditions, and flow velocities were measured using an imaging system and Bernoulli’s equation.

Key Features

  • Simulated artery blockages using 3D-printed models.
  • Visualized flow patterns using a soap film experiment and tracer particles.
  • Calculated pressure differences using Bernoulli’s equation.
  • Analyzed flow velocities and pressure gradients for healthy and unhealthy artery conditions.

Experimental Setup

The experiment setup included:

  • A soap film flow visualization system with two pumps, a container, and a valve.
  • 3D-printed models representing healthy (<30% blockage) and unhealthy (>50% blockage) artery conditions.
  • A high-speed camera (FLIR Blackfly S) and a green laser (LED SOLIS-525C) for tracking tracer particles.
  • 110P8 Hollow Glass Spheres as tracer particles.

Results

The experiment successfully demonstrated the impact of artery blockages on flow patterns and pressure gradients. Key findings include:

  • Flow velocities were measured at different positions (A, B, and C) for both healthy and unhealthy models.
  • Pressure differences were calculated using Bernoulli’s equation, with results showing significant variations between healthy and unhealthy artery conditions.
  • The unhealthy model exhibited higher pressure drops and flow disturbances compared to the healthy model.

Conclusions

The experiment successfully demonstrated the relationship between artery blockages and flow dynamics. The results highlighted the importance of early detection and treatment of arterial blockages to prevent health complications. Future experiments could explore the use of more viscous fluids to better simulate blood flow and improve the accuracy of the results.
