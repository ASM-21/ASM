Audio Equalizer: Analog Filters and Power Amplifier

Abstract

This project involved designing an analog audio equalizer with low-pass, high-pass, and band-pass filters, integrated with a power amplifier. The equalizer allows users to adjust the loudness of individual frequency bands, and the amplifier ensures the output is loud enough for a speaker. The design successfully met all performance specifications, including filter cutoffs, voltage levels, and power output.

Objectives

The primary objectives of the project were:

  • Design and implement low-pass, high-pass, and band-pass filters with specific cutoff frequencies.
  • Provide adjustable controls for each filter and an overall volume control.
  • Integrate a power amplifier to ensure sufficient output power (>400 mW).
  • Meet all voltage and ripple specifications across the frequency range.

Specifications

The circuit was designed to meet the following specifications:

  • Speaker Resistance: 8 Ω
  • Bass Filter (-3dB Cutoff): 320 Hz ±10%
  • Mid Filter (-3dB Bandwidth): 0.32 kHz to 3.2 kHz ±10%
  • Treble Filter (-3dB Cutoff): 3.2 kHz ±10%
  • Output Voltage (Vamp): <15 mVrms (min), 100 mVrms ±10% (max)
  • Output Power: >400 mW from 200 Hz to 10 kHz

Circuit Design

The circuit consists of three main sections:

  1. Filters: Low-pass, high-pass, and band-pass filters were designed using RC circuits. The cutoff frequencies were calculated using the formula: 
    f = 1 / (2πRC)
  2. Volume Control: Potentiometers were used to adjust the gain of each filter and the overall volume. Inverting amplifiers ensured proper signal summation.
  3. Power Amplifier: An LM386D IC was used to amplify the signal, providing sufficient power to drive the speaker.

Results

The circuit successfully met all specifications:

  • Filter Cutoffs: The low-pass, high-pass, and band-pass filters achieved the required cutoff frequencies within ±10% tolerance.
  • Output Voltage: The Vamp values were within the specified range for both minimum and maximum gain settings.
  • Output Power: The amplifier delivered >400 mW of power across the frequency range.
  • Ripple: The ripple voltage was <15 mVrms, meeting the design requirements.

Conclusion

The project successfully demonstrated the design and implementation of an analog audio equalizer. Key takeaways include:

  • The importance of precise component selection and circuit layout to avoid loading effects and signal degradation.
  • The effectiveness of potentiometers for adjustable gain and volume control.
  • The challenges of debugging breadboard circuits and the benefits of soldering for stability.
This project provided valuable hands-on experience with analog circuit design and signal processing, which will be applicable in future engineering projects.
